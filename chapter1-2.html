Concept and use of a classification system

  • Define species.
    • a group of organisms that can reproduce to produce fertile offsprings
  • Describe the different methods that scientists use to classify organisms.
      • studying similarities and differences in morphology (outer appearance)
      • example: presence or absence of wings
      • studying similarities and differences in anatomy (internal structure)
      • example: skeleton
      • studying similarities and differences in DNA base sequences
  • Explain how DNA can be used to identify different species.
      • each species has unique DNA
      • identification is done by comparing base sequences
      • if DNA match with one from a known species, then organism is from that species
      • closely related species have fewer differences in their base sequences
  • What is the purpose of classification systems?
      • to see which organisms are closely related or similar
      • to reflect evolutionary relationships
      • to see the ancestors these organisms have evolved from
      • If two organisms have base sequences in DNA that are very similar, they are more closely related and share a more recent ancestor than other organisms with less similar base sequences.
  • Describe how DNA barcoding might be useful in the conservation of animals, such as myriapods.
      • it is more accurate than traditional methods
      • useful if morphology and anatomy are very similar
      • allows large-scale identification of many species simultaneously
      • only trace samples are required so no need to kill organisms (good for endangered species)
      • so it is more efficient (cheaper and quicker)
      • can identify previously unknown species
