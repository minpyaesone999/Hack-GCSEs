Sexual Reproduction in Plants

  • Define self-pollination.
      • transfer of pollen grains
      • from the anther of a flower to the stigma of the same flower or different flower on the same plant
  • Define cross-pollination.
      • transfer of pollen grains
      • from the anther of a flower to the stigma of a flower on a different plant of the same species
  • Describe the differences between the pollen grains of insect- pollinated and wind-pollinated flowers.
    • insect-pollinated:
      • spiky surface so it can stick to insects or animal bodies
      • larger in size in comparison to wind-pollinated pollen
    • wind-pollinated:
      • small/light
      • smooth surface
  • Describe the differences between the structural adaptions of insect-pollinated and wind-pollinated flowers.
    • wind pollinated:
      • anthers and filaments are hanging outside the flower
      • large anthers â†’ produce large amount of pollen
      • anthers easily release pollen
      • feathery stigma
      • larger surface area stigma to catch pollen
    • insect pollinated:
      • anthers and filaments inside flower to brush against insects
      • sweet smell, bright petals, nectar to attract insects
      • sticky stigma inside flower so pollen from insects stick it
  • Advantages of self pollination
      • parent plants are already adapted to the environment, and they'll pass those useful alleles to their offsprings
      • more chances of fertilization and pollination
      • no need for pollinators (give the names e.g. bees)
      • useful if plants are geographically isolated
      • prevents extinction
  • Disadvantages of self-pollination
      • reduced variation and diversity
      • more likely to be wiped out by infectious disease
      • little ability to adapt to changing conditions
      • increased competition between plants because they all have the same adaptive features
  • Advantages and disadvantages of cross-pollination
      • opposite of the advantages and disadvantages of self-pollination
  • State some features of the flower that attracts pollinators.
      • scent
      • nectar
      • colorful petals
      • large petals
  • State the advantages of seed dispersal.
      • plant species can colonize new areas
      • this reduces competition within species
      • and reduces inbreeding and promotes variation
  • State the role of enzymes in seed germination.
      • break down stored food reserves in seed
      • amylase breaks down starch (named enzyme + substrate)
      • final product is glucose for respiration (product + use)
      • enzymes are also required in the process of respiration
  • Describe the growth of the pollen tube and its entry into the ovule followed by fertilization. Describe the events that occur after pollen grains leave the anther of a flower until fertilization takes place.
      • pollen lands on stigma
      • pollen tube grows
      • through style to ovary
      • male gamete enters ovule
      • through micropyle
      • nuclei of pollen and egg fuse
